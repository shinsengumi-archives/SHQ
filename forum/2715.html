How to speculate like a professional fangirl

Question: "What are the details of Fujita Goro and Shinoda Yaso's
divorce?"

a) Yaso divorced Goro
b) Goro divorced Yaso
c) they divorced *each *other (like millions of people all over the
world) because they couldn't get along
d) there was a vast Aizu conspiracy to destroy the couple and match
Goro with Tokio, even though the domain had nothing to gain from such
a marriage
e) we don't really know

e) is correct

but you can LEARN MORE YOURSELF by looking at the SAITOU HAJIME
TRANSLATIONS right here at our mailing list "FILES" download all the
docs and read it carefully. The translations are from a Japanese
website---this website was chosen specifically because she used the
texts available regarding Saitou Hajime she even bothered to build a
bibliography at her site. Her speculations are clearly separated
from the historical facts (mostly drawn from the work of Shizuko Akama).

you may also use history to help you speculate for example you may
study divorce and marriage in that era and look at the texts available

http://www.hajimenokizu.com/forum/viewtopic.php?p=777#777

alot of things will begin to make sense once you understand the
historical context---the more you read the more your speculation will
change over time---to remain static in your opinion when texts point
to a different direction will not help you (unless you are writing a
work of fiction in which case you can write whatever you want).

*Comes lurking out of the shadows*

Re: How to speculate like a professional fangirl by
Secretary.

I was intrigued by the fact that your friends would
argue for an Aizu conspiracy to split up Denpachi and
Yaso. As you stipulated in your previous essay, and I
do agree with you, when Goro married Tokio, Tokio had
lost practically everything, what had he to gain? What
many people don't like about old-fashioned Japanese
love stories (they are thinking from a modern
perspective on such things) is that Japanese love
stories were often of illicit relationships. A man and
his geisha mistress. A lady separated from her true
love to be married to a lord. But infidelity in any
form, I suppose, is not our 'modern' ideal concept of
true love. Japanese people, as in many other countries
back then, did not marry for love, but for
practicality, especially among the samurai classes.
That's not saying that married couples didn't love
each other, but I know, even from my own grandparents'
experiences, that love had little to do with the
actual concept of marriage back then. Even now,
matchmaking (o-miai in Japanese) is common. When I
described my story as old-fashioned Japanese
romanticism, I was taking these factors into account.
*Then again, I may be biased because I have my own
fictional* interpretation of why Tokio was adopted by
the Kurasawa family, and how the eventual marriage of
Goro and Tokio came about and how Yaso fit into the
picture.(Though whether anyone here is actually
following the story, I just don't know.)

*lurks back into the shadows, but with the very best
wishes for all*
