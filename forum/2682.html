Sorta OT- For all you Aoshi and Misao fans out there- Oniwaban info

by shimazuryu

I know you guys are out there, so here's some info.

The Oniwabanshu was founded in the early 1700s. Tokugawa Yoshimune, the
daimyo of Kii, became the 8th shogun, inaugurating what became the Kyoho
era, and when he came from Kii to Edo, he brought with him a group of 16 men
who were his private agents/bodyguards/investigators. In time, these 16 men
became what's known as the Oniwabanshu, and passed on the tradition within
the same 16 families. This tradition continued all the way through the Edo
period, all the way even into the Bakumatsu. These men were just one of many
groups of investigators in the service of the Shogunate, and took their
orders from the roju (Senior Councilors), wakadoshiyori (Junior Councilors),
and metsuke (inspectors). Their duties, depending on their specific
specialty or classification, ranged from security inside Edo Castle (or,
presumably, wherever the Shogun was, like Nijo Castle) to
patrol/surveillance in Edo. In the Meiji Era, when a concerted effort was
carried out to interview former Shogunate officials, one of those
interviewed was a former Oniwabanshu member-- and his record appears in the
compilation of those interviews, known as "Kyuji Shimonroku"-- loosely, "The
Record of Bygone Days".

(info from Anna Beerens' translation of "Interview with a Bakumatsu
Official")

I wouldn't be surprised if there was interaction between Kondou Isami and
the Oniwabanshu during the Shinsengumi's duty at Nijo Castle, or their work
in the Edo area.

Shinsengumi's friend in time of need- Nagai Naoyuki

by shimazuryu

Nagai Genba no Kami Naoyuki...member of the Wakadoshiyori (junior
councilors') council, Kyoto City Magistrate when Aizu entered in late 1862.
He was featured in the Hijikata special on NHK this month.

http://www.dokidoki.ne.jp/home2/quwatoro/syashin/nagai.jpg　is a
picture of him.

Little did I know that his connection with Shinsengumi-- and in particular
with Kondou Isami-- was deep and personal. The beginning of their
association, as best as I can tell, is when Nagai arranged for a meeting
between Kondou Isami and Goto Shojiro of Tosa, in September of Keio 3
(1867).

Toward the end of their time in Kyoto, Shinsengumi (as Conrad Totman's
"Collapse of the Tokugawa Bakufu") will attest, were on duty at Nijo Castle.
However, through some reason that I don't entirely understand, they had
antagonized the Mito domain (Serizawa Kamo's hometown), and this could have
led to serious problems. It be remembered, I hope, that Ito Kashitaro's
faction was made up of men connected to Mito-- including Ito and Suzuki
Mikisaburo themselves, who were from another domain in Hitachi Province.

Who came to the rescue?

Nagai Naoyuki.

He reassigned Shinsengumi to Fushimi-- which means he was the one who had
them finally leave Kyoto-- and thus spared them from further antagonizing
Mito, which was not only a major Tokugawa collateral domain, but also the
birth clan of the shogun, Yoshinobu.

So-- Shinsengumi is reassigned to Fushimi, but Kondo still makes trips to
Nijo Castle for business. On returning from one of those trips, he was shot
at by Abe Juurou and some of the other survivors of the Ito faction, and
barely made it back to Fushimi in one piece.

It was at this point that-- it's said-- the Shogun Yoshinobu himself came in
person to see the injured Kondou, and gave the Shinsengumi commander the
services of his personal physician, Matsumoto Ryojun. Kondou was apparently
so happy that he cried.

HOWEVER.....there is a rumor that it was not Yoshinobu, but Nagai Naoyuki,
disguised as Yoshinobu, who came to Kondou's bedside and brought Matsumoto
Ryojun with him.

Right now I think that the latter possibility is more likely, especially
given that the Shogun never really seemed to have met anyone face to
face...and that Kondou was in Matsudaira Katamori's charge, not Yoshinobu's
(even though Shinsengumi HAD become hatamoto-- retainers of the Shogun-- in
late 1867).

If it was indeed Nagai Naoyuki, then I am deeply impressed and greatly moved
by his double act of compassion.

Later, Nagai went north with Enomoto's fleet, and he and Nakajima Saburosuke
(former Uraga Magistrate, and also said to be the first Japanese official
who rowed out to Perry's black ships) were chosen as Hakodate Magistrates by
the new Ezo Republic under Enomoto Takeaki, Matsudaira Taro, Ootori Keisuke,
and....Hijikata Toshizo.

(sources: http://www.mirai.ne.jp/~jkj8/bakusin.html,
http://bakusin.jp/eiketu/nagai.html)

-M.

In the interest of balanced coverage-Bio of Kijima Matabei

by shimazuryu

Kijima Matabei- Commander of the Choshu Army at the Kinmon Incident

The manga "Getsumei Seiki" just featured the incident in its newest volume,
and Kijima is there. If this manga is as accurate as it seems to be, then
Kondou saw Kijima go down in battle.

At any rate, here's a bio. Any mistakes I make in translation are my own.

Born Bunka 14, January 8 (February 23, 1817), in Nagato Province, Asa
District, Nishitakadomari Village. 2nd son of Kitamura Masatsune. His
official name was Masahisa, common names were Kamenoshin, Kojiro, and
Matabei. Also known as Mori Kitaro. Pen names: Renki, Souzan, Muan Donin,
Hido.

Though born into the family of the unsalaried courier Kitamura, in Tenpo 7
(1836), he was adopted by Kijima Matabei Masatsune (59 koku 8 sho stipend)
of Ozu District, Tawarayama Village.

Tenpo 8 (1837), the family moved to Mine District, Akayasu Village. Tenpo 12
(1841), Matabei studied kenjutsu under Oishi Susumu of the Yanagawa domain,
and became an expert in the martial arts. In August of Koka 3 (1846), he
went to Edo and studied in the dojo of Kubota Sukeshiro. Returned to Choshu
in Kaei 1 (1848), and succeeded to the Kijima headship in January of Kaei 4
(1851). In November of the same year, he was appointed to the Temawarigumi
(presumably the daimyo's personal entourage), and was made the kago-bugyo
(palanquin overseer) for the daimyo's heir.

In March of Kaei 6, complying with the lord's orders, he went to Edo, and
was there when Perry's ships arrived. In December of the same year, he
submitted a memorial on national defense to the daimyo. Returned to Choshu
in May of Ansei 1, and held several successive jobs there- Official
Examiner, Household Affairs Manager, and others.

October of Bunkyu 2, he was assigned to Edo, and in March of Bunkyu 3
(1863)-- not long after the Shinsengumi-- he went to Kyoto, and in May he
returned to Choshu again, serving as assistant to the Overseer of Stables.
In June he AGAIN returned to Kyoto, at the head of a rifle brigade made up
of hunters. In July he was involved in political affairs, and in September,
returned home.

Before this, Takasugi Shinsaku had organized the Kiheitai, and not long
afterward, on August 18, there was the incident that drove Choshu from
Kyoto. Kijima worked together and formed the Yuugekigun in November.

(Now it gets interesting)
Genji 1 (1864)- March- Resigns as commander of the Yuugekigun, becomes
assistant to the new commander, Kokuji Shinano. *Infiltrates* Kyoto in April
of 1864, returning in May, and taking a strong line in advocating war
(against Aizu/Shinsengumi).

Remember folks-- Choshu knew PRETTY QUICK about Ikedaya...

In June he changed his name, and left for Osaka, leading the Yuugekitai and
Rikishitai (Sumo wrestler brigade) in battle at the Kinmon Incident in July.
While leading the forces from Saga-Tenryuji Temple to Hamagurigomon (outer
gate at the Imperial Palace), he was shot at by Aizu forces, and his nephew
Kitamura Takeshichi acted as his kaishaku (beheading at seppuku). Age at
death was 49.

Court rank-- Senior 4th rank. Buried at Higashiyama-Reizan in Kyoto, as well
as in Hikari City (in the former Choshu) at Iwabiyama Shrine, as well as in
his former home of Mine (pronounced "Mee-neh") City, West Akayasu.

(Source:
http://ddb.libnet.kulib.kyoto-u.ac.jp/exhibit/ishin/jinmei/Kijima.html)

-M.
